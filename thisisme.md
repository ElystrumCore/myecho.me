# This Is Me

**CJ Elliott** — Grande Prairie, Alberta

---

## The Arc

Pipefitter apprentice at 19. SVP Projects by 38. Twenty years in oil and gas construction — not in an office, on the ground. Welding tickets, safety tickets, H2S tickets, then lead hand, then foreman, then superintendent, then the letters after the name that mean you stopped swinging the hammer and started directing the people who do.

The career path looks like a ladder from outside. From inside, it was a series of problems that nobody else wanted to solve. Every rung was a system that was broken and a person who said "you figure it out."

Pipeline tie-ins at -40. Turnaround schedules that existed on napkins. Multi-million dollar projects managed in spreadsheets by people who didn't trust software because the last three tools they were given didn't work. The tools didn't work because the people who built them had never been on a lease road.

That's the gap. The people who build the technology don't know the industry. The people who know the industry don't build technology. I ended up in the middle.

## The Pivot

The AI moment hit different when you've been running construction projects for two decades. Every other tech wave — ERPs, IoT, drones, digital twins — promised to revolutionize the field and delivered a nicer spreadsheet. AI was different. Not because the marketing said so. Because I could see what it could actually do with the data we already had.

18,000 B Pressure Welder certifications in Alberta. 4,614 CWB inspectors across Western Canada. 875 active ABSA permit programs. 600,000+ AER well records. This data existed in PDFs, in government databases, in filing cabinets, in people's heads. Nobody had connected it. Nobody had built a system that could tell a service company "here are the 43 inspectors within 200km of your jobsite who hold the endorsement your project requires, ranked by availability."

So I started building that system.

## What I Believe

**The gap between the field and the office is the most expensive problem in industrial construction.** Every cost overrun, every schedule slip, every safety incident traces back to information that existed somewhere but didn't reach the person who needed it in time. AI doesn't fix this by replacing people — it fixes it by connecting the data that's already there.

**Local intelligence beats general intelligence.** A model trained on Grande Prairie's drilling patterns is worth more to a Grande Prairie contractor than GPT-5. The data is specific, the relationships are specific, the decisions are specific. The companies that win are the ones with the best local data, not the biggest models.

**The trades workforce is underserved by technology.** A journeyman pipefitter with 15 years of experience and a perfect safety record has no digital identity beyond a paper resume and a stack of certification cards. That's a solvable problem, and solving it creates a marketplace that doesn't exist yet.

**Building is learning.** I didn't study AI in a classroom. I built 27 interconnected projects across 42 Docker services, trained a custom language model from scratch, implemented a Kuramoto coupled oscillator substrate, and validated a harmonic codec against a live corpus. The theory came from the building, not the other way around.

**Energy creates intelligence creates coordination creates money.** That's the operating law. Raw signal becomes structured knowledge becomes organized action becomes revenue. Every system I build follows this flow.
